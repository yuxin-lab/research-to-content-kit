# Compliance Wording Examples

This document provides examples of safer wording for medical, health, skincare, biotechnology, and science communication content.

It is designed for education, documentation, and public-facing communication workflows.

## Important note

This document does not provide legal, regulatory, or medical advice.

Users should follow applicable laws, platform rules, product category requirements, and professional standards.

## 1. Acne-related communication

### Risky wording

This product treats acne.

### Why it may be risky

This wording may sound like a disease treatment claim.

### Safer wording

This content discusses acne-related skin care from a science communication perspective.

---

### Risky wording

This ingredient eliminates acne inflammation.

### Why it may be risky

This wording may overstate a mechanism and may sound like a drug-like effect claim.

### Safer wording

Research suggests that inflammatory pathways may be related to acne development.

---

### Risky wording

Use this and acne will disappear.

### Why it may be risky

This wording implies a guaranteed result.

### Safer wording

Individual skin conditions vary, and repeated or severe acne may require professional evaluation.

## 2. Skin barrier communication

### Risky wording

This product completely repairs the skin barrier.

### Why it may be risky

This wording implies a complete and guaranteed repair effect.

### Safer wording

This product is positioned for daily skin care and barrier-support communication.

---

### Risky wording

This method rebuilds damaged skin.

### Why it may be risky

This wording may imply a strong medical or regenerative effect.

### Safer wording

This content explains skin barrier care from a public education perspective.

## 3. Anti-aging communication

### Risky wording

This product reverses aging.

### Why it may be risky

This wording may overstate the effect and imply a guaranteed biological reversal.

### Safer wording

This content discusses aging-related skin changes and daily care concepts.

---

### Risky wording

Clinically proven to remove wrinkles.

### Why it may be risky

This wording may imply a specific clinical effect and needs strong evidence and category-appropriate support.

### Safer wording

The wording should match the product category, evidence level, and applicable platform rules.

## 4. Nutrition-related communication

### Risky wording

Sugar causes acne.

### Why it may be risky

This wording turns a possible association into a direct causal claim.

### Safer wording

High-glycemic diets may be associated with acne in some people.

---

### Risky wording

Stop sugar and your acne will be cured.

### Why it may be risky

This wording implies a guaranteed dietary treatment effect.

### Safer wording

Diet may be one of several factors related to acne-prone skin, but individual situations vary.

## 5. Biotechnology background communication

### Risky wording

This technology treats inflammation at the gene level.

### Why it may be risky

This wording may sound like a drug or medical treatment claim, especially in product promotion.

### Safer wording

This technology background can be discussed from a research and science communication perspective.

---

### Risky wording

This product uses drug-level biotechnology to solve skin problems.

### Why it may be risky

This wording may blur the boundary between research background and product effect.

### Safer wording

The product communication should clearly separate scientific background from product category-appropriate claims.

## 6. Expert collaboration wording

### Risky wording

Doctor-developed product that guarantees results.

### Why it may be risky

This wording combines expert endorsement with guaranteed effect claims.

### Safer wording

Developed with reference to expert communication insights and research background.

---

### Risky wording

The expert proves this product works.

### Why it may be risky

This wording may overstate the expert role and imply direct endorsement or proof.

### Safer wording

The expert perspective helps explain the scientific background of this topic.

## 7. General safer wording bank

Possible safer expressions:

- May be related to
- May help explain
- Is associated with
- Supports public education
- From a science communication perspective
- Based on research background
- Designed for daily care communication
- Individual situations vary
- Further evaluation may be needed
- Does not replace professional medical advice
- Product claims should match the applicable product category

## 8. Review checklist

Before publishing content, check:

- Does the content imply diagnosis?
- Does the content imply treatment?
- Does the content imply guaranteed results?
- Does the content overstate scientific evidence?
- Does the content confuse research background with product effect?
- Does the content use expert identity in a misleading way?
- Does the content match the intended platform and product category?
