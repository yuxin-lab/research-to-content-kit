# Slide Outline Template

This template helps convert research materials, expert notes, or science communication topics into a structured slide deck outline.

It is designed for public education, expert presentations, medical science communication, and content planning.

## 1. Basic information

### Topic

Write the topic here.

### Source material

List the source materials:

- Research paper
- Expert interview
- Clinical notes
- Public education article
- Internal training material
- Product education document
- Other

### Target audience

Examples:

- General public
- Patients
- Students
- Clinicians
- Internal team
- Health content creators
- Product education teams

### Presentation goal

Examples:

- Explain a scientific concept
- Introduce a research finding
- Educate users
- Support short video creation
- Prepare a public lecture
- Structure expert knowledge

## 2. Core message

Write the main message of the slide deck in one sentence.

Example:

> Acne is not only a surface skin problem; it may involve sebum, follicle blockage, inflammation, lifestyle factors, and long-term skin management.

## 3. Slide structure

### Slide 1: Title

Write the title and subtitle.

### Slide 2: Audience pain point

Describe the common problem or misunderstanding.

### Slide 3: Background

Explain why this topic matters.

### Slide 4: Key concept

Introduce the central scientific concept.

### Slide 5: Mechanism explanation

Explain the mechanism in simple language.

### Slide 6: Evidence or research finding

Summarize the supporting research or expert observation.

### Slide 7: Daily-life interpretation

Connect the science to real-life situations.

### Slide 8: Common mistakes

List common misunderstandings or wrong approaches.

### Slide 9: Practical suggestions

Provide general, non-medical education suggestions.

### Slide 10: Summary

Repeat the key message in a simple way.

## 4. Visual suggestions

Possible visual formats:

- Timeline
- Flowchart
- Before-and-after concept diagram
- Mechanism diagram
- Expert quote card
- Research paper screenshot
- 3D animation frame
- Data chart
- Case-style scenario
- Q&A slide

## 5. Wording risk review

Check whether the slides include:

- Disease treatment claims
- Guaranteed results
- Overstated scientific claims
- Product effect claims without proper support
- Confusion between research background and product function
- Expert endorsement language that may be misleading

## 6. Safer wording examples

### Instead of:

This method cures acne.

### Consider:

This slide explains acne-related mechanisms from a science education perspective.

---

### Instead of:

This product repairs the skin barrier completely.

### Consider:

This product is positioned for daily skin care and barrier-support communication.

---

### Instead of:

The expert proves this effect.

### Consider:

The expert perspective helps explain the scientific background and communication logic.
